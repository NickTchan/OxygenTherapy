# British Thoracic Society Guideline for Oxygen Use in Adults in Healthcare and Emergency Settings (2017)

> **Source**: O'Driscoll BR, Howard LS, Earis J, Mak V, on behalf of the British Thoracic Society Emergency Oxygen Guideline Group. British Thoracic Society Guideline for oxygen use in adults in healthcare and emergency settings. *BMJ Open Respiratory Research*. 2017;4:e000170. doi:10.1136/bmjresp-2016-000170

## Executive Summary: Philosophy of the Guideline

- Oxygen is a treatment for **hypoxaemia**, not breathlessness. Oxygen has not been proven to have any consistent effect on the sensation of breathlessness in non-hypoxaemic patients `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)`.
- The essence of this guideline: oxygen should be prescribed according to a **target saturation range** and those who administer oxygen therapy should monitor the patient and keep within the target saturation range `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)`.
- The guideline recommends aiming to achieve **normal or near-normal oxygen saturation** for all acutely ill patients apart from those at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure or those receiving terminal palliative care `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)`.

## Key Recommendations Summary

### A. Achieving Desirable Oxygen Saturation Ranges in Acute Illness

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| A1 | Aim to achieve normal or near-normal oxygen saturation for all acutely ill patients apart from those at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| A2 | Recommended target saturation range for acutely ill patients **not at risk** of hypercapnic respiratory failure: **94–98%** | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| A3 | For patients with known **COPD or other risk factors** for hypercapnic respiratory failure (morbid obesity, cystic fibrosis, chest wall deformities, neuromuscular disorders, fixed airflow obstruction): target saturation **88–92%** pending blood gas results | A (COPD), D (other) | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| A4 | Most non-hypoxaemic breathless patients do not benefit from oxygen therapy; a sudden reduction of ≥3% in oxygen saturation within target range should prompt fuller assessment | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| A5 | Fully conscious hypoxaemic patients should ideally be allowed to maintain the most upright posture possible unless immobilisation is required | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### B. Clinical and Laboratory Assessment

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| B1 | Fully trained clinicians should assess all acutely ill patients by measuring respiratory rate, pulse, BP, temperature, circulating blood volume and anaemia; seek expert assistance early for major life-threatening illnesses | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| B2 | Oxygen saturation ('the fifth vital sign') should be checked by trained staff using pulse oximetry in all breathless and acutely ill patients; record inspired oxygen device and flow rate on observation chart | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| B3 | Initial clinical assessment should include use of a recognised physiological 'track and trigger' system such as NEWS | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| B4 | For patients at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure, use the relevant section of the 2017 NEWS chart; points awarded if SpO₂ is below OR above target range | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### C. Arterial and Capillary Blood Gases

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| C1 | For critically ill patients or those with shock/hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg), initial blood gas should be arterial. For most patients, either ABG or arterialised earlobe blood gases may be used for accurate pH and PCO₂; PO₂ less accurate in earlobe samples | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| C2 | Local anaesthesia should be used for all ABG specimens except in emergencies | A | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| C3 | Blood gases should be checked in: all critically ill patients; unexpected fall in SpO₂ <94%; deteriorating SpO₂ in chronic hypoxaemia; patients requiring increased FiO₂; patients at risk of hypercapnia with acute breathlessness; patients at risk of metabolic acidosis; any other evidence blood gases would be useful | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### D. Initial Oxygen Therapy: Equipment Choice (Non-Critical Illness)

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| D1 | For acutely breathless patients **not at risk** of hypercapnic respiratory failure with SpO₂ <85%: start with reservoir mask at 15 L/min; adjust downwards (nasal cannulae 1–6 L/min or simple face mask 5–10 L/min) to maintain target 94–98% once stabilised | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| D2 | In other cases of acute hypoxaemia without critical illness or risk factors for hypercapnia: start with nasal cannulae (or simple face mask if not tolerated) with flow adjusted to achieve saturation 94–98% | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| D3 | If medium-concentration therapy does not achieve desired saturation, change to reservoir mask and seek senior/specialist advice | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### E. Oxygen Therapy in Critical Illness

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| E1 | Use highest feasible inspired oxygen for ventilation during CPR. Once spontaneous circulation restored and SpO₂ can be monitored reliably, aim for target 94–98%; take ABG to guide ongoing therapy. If hypercapnic respiratory failure, reset target to 88–92% or consider mechanical ventilation | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| E2 | In critical illness (major trauma, sepsis, shock, anaphylaxis): initiate with reservoir mask at 15 L/min, aim at saturation 94–98%. This applies to patients with risk factors for hypercapnia pending blood gas results and expert assessment | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| E3 | In drowning: aim at oxygen saturation 94–98% once spontaneous circulation restored | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| E4 | In acute seizures (epilepsy or other causes): high-concentration oxygen until satisfactory oximetry obtained, then aim for 94–98% or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| E5 | In major head injury: aim at oxygen saturation 94–98%. Initial treatment: high-concentration oxygen from reservoir mask at 15 L/min pending blood gas results or until airway secured by intubation | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| E6 | In carbon monoxide poisoning: aim at oxygen saturation 100% and use reservoir mask at 15 L/min irrespective of oximeter reading and PaO₂ (oximetry cannot differentiate carboxyhaemoglobin from oxyhaemoglobin) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### F. Oxygen Therapy for Specific Conditions

#### Respiratory Conditions (Low Risk of Hypercapnic Respiratory Failure)

| Condition | Target SpO₂ | Recommendation | Grade | Citation |
|-----------|------------|---------------|--------|----------|
| Acute asthma | 94–98% | F1 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Pneumonia (not at risk of hypercapnia) | 94–98% | F2 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Lung cancer/breathlessness | 94–98% (unless coexisting COPD) | F3 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Pulmonary fibrosis/interstitial lung disease deterioration | 94–98% or highest possible if targets cannot be achieved | F4 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Pneumothorax | 94–98% | F5 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Pneumothorax requiring hospital observation without drainage | High-concentration oxygen (15 L/min via reservoir mask) unless at risk of hypercapnia | F6 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Pleural effusion | 94–98% (or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia) | F7 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Pulmonary embolism | 94–98% (or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia) | F8 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

#### Non-Respiratory Conditions

| Condition | Target SpO₂ | Recommendation | Grade | Citation |
|-----------|------------|---------------|--------|----------|
| Acute heart failure | 94–98% (or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia) | F9 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Cardiogenic pulmonary oedema not responding to standard treatment | CPAP with entrained oxygen or high-flow humidified nasal oxygen to maintain saturation 94–98% (or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia) | F10 | B | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Anaemia | 94–98% or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia | F11 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Sickle cell crisis/acute chest syndrome | 94–98% or aim at saturation level usual for individual patient | F12 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Myocardial infarction/acute coronary syndromes | 94–98% or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia | F13 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Stroke | Avoid high concentrations unless required to maintain normal saturation; aim 94–98% or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia | F14 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Most poisonings | 94–98% unless at risk of hypercapnia | F15 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Paraquat or bleomycin poisoning | Give oxygen only if saturation falls below 85%; reduce/stop if rises above 88% | F16 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Most metabolic/renal disorders | 94–98% unless at risk of hypercapnia | F17 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Cluster headaches | Oxygen at flow ≥12 L/min from reservoir mask; home oxygen should be provided | F18 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### G. Patients at Risk of Hypercapnic Respiratory Failure

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| G1 (=A3) | For most patients with known COPD or other known risk factors for hypercapnic respiratory failure: target saturation **88–92%** pending blood gas results | A (COPD), D (other) | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| G2 | Patients with prior hypercapnic failure (requiring NIV or IPPV) without alert card: start low-concentration oxygen using 24% Venturi mask at 2–3 L/min (or 28% Venturi at 4 L/min or nasal cannulae 1–2 L/min); target 88–92% pending urgent blood gases; treat as high priority; reduce oxygen if saturation >92%, increase if <88% | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| G3 | Initial oxygen treatment of CF exacerbations similar to COPD exacerbations: target saturation 88–92% | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| G4 | Initial management of musculoskeletal/neurological disorders with acute/acute-on-chronic respiratory failure: aim at 88–92% and measure blood gases to determine if NIV required | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| G5 | Morbidly obese patients (BMI >40 kg/m²), even without OSA: at risk of hypoventilation; give titrated oxygen to maintain target 88–92% | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| G6 | NIV should be considered for hypercapnic patients with COPD, CF, neuromuscular disorders or morbid obesity at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure if pH <7.35 or [H⁺] >45 nmol/L | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### H. Oxygen Use During Pregnancy

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| H1 | Women with major trauma, sepsis or acute illness during pregnancy: same oxygen therapy as other seriously ill patients, target 94–98%. Same target for hypoxaemia due to acute pregnancy complications | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| H2 | Women with underlying hypoxaemic conditions: supplemental oxygen during labour to achieve 94–98% unless at risk of hypercapnia (target 88–92%) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| H3 | Fully conscious pregnant women with no cardiovascular compromise: may be managed in sitting position or full left lateral position if lying down | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| H4 | Pregnant women >20 weeks gestation at risk of cardiovascular compromise: position to avoid aortocaval compression using left lateral tilt, manual uterine displacement or full left lateral position | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| H5 | Women >20 weeks pregnant with hypoxaemia associated with reduced consciousness or requiring respiratory/cardiovascular support/CPR: manage with left lateral tilt or manual uterine displacement to improve cardiac output and oxygen delivery | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| H6 | Oxygen supplementation during intrauterine fetal resuscitation during labour: only required when evidence of maternal hypoxaemia (SpO₂ <94%) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### J. Oxygen Use in Perioperative Care and Procedures Requiring Conscious Sedation

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| J1 | Hyperoxaemia not recommended routinely in perioperative/postoperative period to reduce incidence of postoperative nausea and vomiting | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| J2 | All procedures involving conscious sedation warrant routine continuous monitoring of SpO₂ via pulse oximetry prior to, during, and in recovery period, particularly fibre-optic bronchoscopy and upper GI endoscopy | C | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| J3 | Significant arterial oxygen desaturation (SpO₂ <90% or fall ≥4% prolonged >1 min during endoscopy) should be corrected by supplemental oxygen to achieve target 94–98% or 88–92% in those at risk of hypercapnia | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| J4 | Complicated upper GI endoscopy or procedures in patients with cardiorespiratory comorbidity: blood gases should be measured if prolonged oxygen administration required; routine oxygen administration not recommended as may delay recognition of respiratory failure | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| J5 | Constant clinical assessment crucial at all stages of conscious sedation; monitoring of capnography or transcutaneous CO₂ may be useful adjunct to identify early respiratory depression | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| J6 | During recovery after procedures requiring conscious sedation: supplemental oxygen titrated to achieve target 94–98% in most patients, 88–92% in those at risk of hypercapnia | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### K. Oxygen Use in Palliative Care

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| K1 | Oxygen use in palliative care patients restricted to patients with SpO₂ consistently <90% or patients who report significant relief of breathlessness from oxygen. In non-hypoxaemic patients, opioids and non-pharmacological measures should be tried before oxygen | B | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| K2 | In general, no role for monitoring oxygen saturation or PaO₂ in comfort-focused care in last few days of life. If patient appears comfortable, oxygen levels irrelevant and should not influence care | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### L. Mixtures of Oxygen with Other Gases (Heliox and Entonox)

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| L1 | Insufficient evidence to support use of Heliox either as inhaled gas or as driving gas for nebuliser therapy in adult patients with acute exacerbations of asthma or AECOPD except as part of randomised clinical trials or in exceptional circumstances | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| L2 | Therapeutic trial of Heliox reasonable in patients with mechanical upper airway obstruction or postoperative stridor | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| L3 | Heliox use for patients with asthma or COPD should be considered only in clinical trials or in specialist hands for severe exacerbations not responding to standard treatment (and in COPD patients where contraindications to intubation) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| M1 | Use of Entonox gas mixture for analgesia should be avoided if possible in patients at risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### N. CPAP and Humidified High-Flow Nasal Oxygen

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| N1 | Patients with diagnosed sleep-disordered breathing established on CPAP undergoing surgery should bring machines and use them in preoperative and postoperative period. If adequate saturations not achieved despite CPAP, assess for worsening ventilation with blood gases and entrain oxygen to achieve saturation 88–92% | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| N2 | CPAP with entrained oxygen to maintain saturation 94–98% should be considered as adjunctive treatment to improve gas exchange in patients with cardiogenic pulmonary oedema not responding to standard treatment in hospital or prehospital care | B | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### P. Patients with Tracheostomy or Laryngectomy

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| P1 | When oxygen required by patients with prior tracheostomy or laryngectomy: tracheostomy mask (varying flow as necessary) should achieve desired oxygen saturation. Alternative delivery device (usually T-piece fitted directly to tracheostomy tube) may be necessary if patient deteriorates | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### Q. Humidification of Oxygen

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| Q1 | Humidification not required for delivery of low-flow oxygen (mask or nasal cannulae) or short-term use of high-flow oxygen. Not required in prehospital care. Pending clinical trials, reasonable to use humidified oxygen for patients requiring high-flow systems >24 hours or who report upper airway discomfort due to dryness | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Q2 | In emergency situation, humidified oxygen use can be confined to patients with tracheostomy or artificial airway, although these patients can be managed without humidification for short periods | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Q3 | Humidification may benefit patients with viscous secretions causing difficulty with expectoration; benefit can be achieved using nebulised normal saline | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| Q4 | Bubble bottles which allow oxygen to bubble through container of water should not be used: no evidence of clinically significant benefit but risk of infection | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### R. Driving Gas for Nebulised Treatments

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| R1 | For patients with asthma: nebulisers should be driven by piped oxygen or from oxygen cylinder fitted with high-flow regulator capable of delivering flow >6 L/min. Patient changed back to usual oxygen mask/cannulae when nebuliser therapy complete. If cylinder does not produce this flow rate, use air-driven nebuliser with supplemental oxygen by nasal cannulae at 2–6 L/min to maintain appropriate saturation | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| R2 | When nebulised bronchodilators given to patients with hypercapnic acidosis: use ultrasonic nebuliser or jet nebuliser driven by compressed air; if necessary, give supplementary oxygen concurrently by nasal cannulae to maintain saturation 88–92%. Same precautions for patients at risk of hypercapnia prior to blood gas results; monitor SpO₂ continuously during treatment. Once nebulised treatment completed, reinstitute previous targeted oxygen therapy | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### S. Prescribing Oxygen Therapy

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| S1 | Every healthcare facility should have standard oxygen prescription document or, preferably, designated oxygen section on all drug-prescribing cards or guided prescription in electronic prescribing systems | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| S2 | Prescription for oxygen should always be provided, except in sudden illness when must be started immediately and documented retrospectively | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| S3 | Doctors and other prescribers should prescribe oxygen using target saturation range and sign drug chart or electronic prescribing order | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| S4 | Oxygen target saturation range should be prescribed for all patients admitted to hospital to ensure appropriate therapy if required and that all clinicians aware of appropriate target range for every patient | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### T. Monitoring and Adjusting Oxygen Therapy

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| T1 | Pulse oximetry must be available in all locations where emergency oxygen is being used by healthcare professionals | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| T2 | All documents recording oximetry measurements or blood gas results should state whether patient breathing air or specified oxygen delivery device and flow rate using abbreviations in Table 5 | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| T3 | In all situations where repeated blood gas measurements required, measure as soon as possible, usually within 30 min of any treatment change, to determine if proposed target saturations appropriate. Consider use of indwelling arterial catheter if multiple samples likely required | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| T4 | Adjustments should only be made by registered staff trained to administer oxygen. If SpO₂ falls below prespecified range, increase oxygen concentration; if rises above range, reduce concentration. If monitoring performed by unregistered staff, clear protocol must require they inform trained staff if SpO₂ above or below target | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### U. Weaning and Discontinuation of Oxygen Therapy

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| U1 | Lower oxygen concentration if patient clinically stable and SpO₂ above target range or if in upper zone of target range for some time (usually 4–8 hours) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U2 | If target saturation maintained, continue new delivery system and flow. Repeat blood gas measurements not required. If patient stable, process can be repeated and patient eventually weaned off oxygen | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U3 | Most stable convalescent patients eventually stepped down to 2 L/min via nasal cannulae prior to cessation. Patients at risk of hypercapnia may be stepped down to 1 L/min (or occasionally 0.5 L/min) via nasal cannulae or 24% Venturi mask at 2 L/min as lowest concentration prior to cessation | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U4 | Oxygen therapy should be stopped once patient clinically stable on low-concentration oxygen and SpO₂ within desired range on two consecutive observations (but prescription for target range should remain active in case of future deterioration). May be appropriate to alter target range following senior review in patients with chronic cardiopulmonary disease who have saturations <94% when stable or deemed sensible to discharge with saturations <94% pending outpatient oxygen assessment. Oxygen also stopped if patient come to end of written protocol of timed oxygen (e.g., postoperatively) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U5 | SpO₂ on air should be monitored for 5 min after stopping oxygen therapy. If remains in desired range, recheck at 1 hour | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U6 | If SpO₂ and physiological 'track and trigger' score (e.g., NEWS) satisfactory at 1 hour, patient has safely discontinued oxygen therapy. However, saturation and physiology should continue to be monitored regularly according to patient's underlying clinical condition | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U7 | If saturation falls below patient's target range on stopping oxygen therapy, restart lowest concentration that maintained patient in target range and monitor for 5 min. If restores saturation into target range, continue oxygen at this level and attempt discontinuation again at later date provided patient remains clinically stable | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U8 | If patient requires oxygen therapy to be restarted at higher concentration than before to maintain same target saturation range, patient should have clinical review to establish cause for deterioration | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| U9 | Some patients may have episodic hypoxaemia (e.g., after minor exertion or due to mucus plugging) after safely discontinuing oxygen therapy. Ongoing prescription for target saturation range will allow these patients to receive oxygen as need arises but transient asymptomatic desaturation does not require correction | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### V. Practical Aspects of Oxygen Use

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| V1 | Emergency oxygen should be available in primary care medical centres, preferably using oxygen cylinders with integral high-flow regulators. Alternatively, oxygen cylinders fitted with high-flow regulators (delivering up to 15 L/min) must be used to allow use with reservoir masks | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| V2 | Healthcare organisations should take measures to eliminate risk of oxygen tubing being connected to incorrect wall oxygen outlet or to outlets delivering compressed air or other gases instead of oxygen. Air flow meters should be removed from wall sockets or covered with designated air outlet cover when not in use. Special care should be taken if twin oxygen outlets in use | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

### W. Practical Aspects of Oxygen Dispensing, Documentation and Monitoring

| Recommendation | Content | Grade | Citation |
|---------------|---------|-------|----------|
| W1 | Registered nurses and others who dispense drugs in hospitals should sign drug chart or electronic prescribing record at every drug round and check patient receiving oxygen therapy. To check patient within target saturation and also to check whether weaning and discontinuation should be instituted | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W2 | Most patients prescribed oxygen target range. If patients on air at time of drug round, registered nurses should sign drug chart using code such as 'A' for air and observation chart should also be filled in using code A for air | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W3 | All patients should have SpO₂ observed for at least 5 min after starting oxygen therapy or for patients requiring increased concentration of oxygen and after oxygen therapy decreased or stopped | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W4 | If SpO₂ above target saturation range and patient stable, delivery system or oxygen flow rate should be modified to return saturation to within target range | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W5 | Patients with target saturation 88–92% should have blood gases measured within 30–60 min to ensure carbon dioxide level not rising. Also applies to those at risk of developing hypercapnic respiratory failure but who have normal PCO₂ on initial blood gas measurement | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W6 | Stable patients whose SpO₂ within target range 94–98% do not need repeat blood gas measurements within 30–60 min if no risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure and acidosis and may not need any further blood gas measurements unless further deterioration including symptoms/signs of possible hypercapnia | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W7 | Stable patients on oxygen treatment should have SpO₂ and physiological variables (e.g., NEWS) measured four times a day | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W8 | In those with signs of critical illness (e.g., NEWS ≥7), SpO₂ should be monitored continuously and patient may require level 2 or 3 care on HDU or critical care unit | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W9 | If patient clinically stable and SpO₂ within target range, treatment should be continued (or eventually lowered) depending on clinical situation | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W10 | Oxygen therapy should be increased if saturation below desired range and decreased if saturation above desired range (and eventually discontinued as patient recovers) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W11 | New saturation (and new delivery system) and flow rate should be recorded on patient's observation chart after 5 min of treatment at new oxygen concentration. Each change should be recorded by clinician trained to administer oxygen by signing observation chart (only changes should be signed for) | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W12 | Repeat blood gas measurements not required for stable patients who require reduced concentration of oxygen (or cessation of oxygen therapy) to maintain desired target saturation | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W13 | Patients with no risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure do not always need repeat blood gas measurements after increase in oxygen concentration. However, patient requires clinical review to determine why SpO₂ has fallen | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

| W15 | For patients with no risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure, monitoring by pulse oximeter sufficient (repeated blood gases not required) provided patient clinically stable and SpO₂ remains in desired range, usually 94–98% | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |
| W16 | If patient's SpO₂ lower than prescribed target range, first check all aspects of oxygen delivery system and oximeter device for faults or errors | D | `(O'Driscoll et al., 2017)` |

# Thoracic Society of Australia and New Zealand Position Statement on Acute Oxygen Use in Adults: 'Swimming between the flags' (2022)

> **Source**: Barnett A, Beasley R, Buchan C, Chien J, Farah CS, King G, et al. Thoracic Society of Australia and New Zealand Position Statement on Acute Oxygen Use in Adults: 'Swimming between the flags'. *Respirology*. 2022;27:262-276. doi:10.1111/resp.14218

## Abstract
Oxygen is a life-saving therapy but, when given inappropriately, may also be hazardous. Therefore, in the acute medical setting, oxygen should only be given as treatment for hypoxaemia and requires appropriate prescription, monitoring and review. This update to the TSANZ guidance on acute oxygen therapy is a brief and practical resource for all healthcare workers involved with administering oxygen therapy to adults in the acute medical setting. It does not apply to intubated or paediatric patients `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

## Concepts Behind Key Recommendations (Table 2)

### (1) Assess Oxygenation
- Hypoxaemia is an independent marker of risk and of poor outcomes, due to the severity of the underlying disease(s) that caused hypoxaemia `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- There are clinically significant limitations to the interpretation of pulse oximetry such that ABG should be taken in the appropriate clinical situations `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### (2) Oxygen is a Drug and Thus Requires Prescription
- Oxygen is a drug and should be prescribed for the relief of hypoxaemia, and not breathlessness in the absence of hypoxaemia `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Oxygen can be initiated or the flow rate increased acutely when needed, but as applies to any emergency situation, this requires urgent medical review `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### (3) Recommended SpO₂ Targets
- There are risks associated with both hypoxaemia and hyperoxaemia, which underlie the importance of prescribing oxygen, only if required, to a specific target oxygen saturation range ('swimming between the flags' concept) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- The lower limits of safety for PaO₂ and SpO₂ are variable and depend on the clinical scenario. However, a PaO₂ of at least 50 mmHg and SpO₂ of at least 80% is likely to prevent immediate death from hypoxaemia `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- There is now strong evidence supporting both lower and upper SpO₂ target ranges in acute respiratory and other medical conditions `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Supplemental oxygen should not be used to achieve SpO₂ above the recommended target range, with the intent of protecting against future hypoxaemia in the setting of a clinical deterioration. This may mask any subsequent deterioration in SpO₂ delaying recognition of deterioration, senior clinician review and escalation of appropriate therapy `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### (4) Delivery
- Nasal cannulae are the preferred method of oxygen delivery for most patients because they can remain in situ during delivery of bronchodilators by inhaler and spacer, eating and other activities `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- hNHF-O₂ delivers high FiO₂ (up to 1.0) at high-flow rates (up to 70 L/min). It is therefore an effective oxygen delivery system for acute, severe hypoxaemic respiratory failure `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### (5) High FiO₂ to Achieve Target SpO₂ Indicates Serious Illness
- Early warning systems are effective in picking up deterioration earlier, particularly when they combine oxygen delivery (FiO₂ or flow rate), with the resultant SpO₂ `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Increasing respiratory rate is also a highly sensitive marker of deterioration `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Increasing FiO₂ requirements to maintain target saturations indicates deterioration. High FiO₂ to maintain adequate oxygen saturations means that should further deterioration occur, higher level respiratory support is then urgently required to avoid life-threatening hypoxaemia `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### (6) Acute Respiratory Acidosis
- This indicates severe physiological derangement (inadequate alveolar ventilation to clear CO₂) and has a number of potential causes. Augmentation of ventilatory support may be required through invasive or NIV `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- NIV is not usually indicated in the absence of acute respiratory acidosis `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

## Detailed Recommendations with Practice Points

### 1. Assess Oxygenation

**I. Pulse oximetry is a 'vital sign' to be considered together with other signs, including respiratory rate, and is a predictor of potentially serious clinical events. Pulse oximetry should therefore be available in all clinical situations in which oxygen is used (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

*Practice points:*
- a. There is variable accuracy of pulse oximetry to predict SaO₂ (arterial oxygen saturation [measured by arterial blood gas]) in acutely ill patients, with SpO₂ measurements both over- and under-estimating SaO₂, with wide limits of agreement. The accuracy of SpO₂ may worsen with factors including disease severity and patients' physical characteristics. Clinicians need to be aware of, and take into account the variable accuracy of SpO₂ in using pulse oximetry in clinical practice and measure ABGs when appropriate `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- b. An SpO₂ of ≥92% is a practically lower threshold to rule out hypoxaemia, defined as an SaO₂ <90% or an arterial partial pressure of oxygen (PaO₂) <60 mmHg (8 kPa). However, SaO₂ may be underestimated by up to 8% and an SaO₂ <90% may be missed in up to 28% `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- c. There should always be clinical judgement when interpreting SpO₂ values, and to avoid over-reliance on pulse oximeters. In particular, when diagnosis, severity assessment and treatment are dependent on oxygen status, ABG may be necessary `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- d. In the immediate assessment of an acutely unwell patient, oxygen saturations should be measured by oximetry, pending the availability of ABG if required `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

**II. ABG measurement should be considered in the following situations (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`:**
- Critically ill patients with cardiorespiratory or metabolic dysfunction
- In patients with an SpO₂ <92% in whom hypoxaemia may be present
- Deteriorating SpO₂ requiring increased FiO₂
- Patients at risk of hypercapnia
- Patients with symptoms or signs compatible with acute respiratory disease in whom a reliable oximetry signal cannot be obtained

*Practice points:*
- a. Hypoxaemia requires investigation and treatment of the underlying cause(s), and consideration of the contribution of hypoventilation, including measurement of arterial partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO₂) and pH `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- b. Peripheral venous blood gas (VBG) analysis is a less invasive test; however, it does not provide an accurate estimate of PaCO₂ or PaO₂. It does, however, provide rapid clinically important information to assess acutely unwell patients, including pH, lactate, glucose, haemoglobin, sodium and potassium. A venous PCO₂ of <40 mmHg makes hypercapnia unlikely, but does not rule it out. Therefore, exclusion of hypercapnia, when clinically relevant, requires ABG to be measured `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- c. Arterialised capillary earlobe or fingertip blood gas measurements represent an alternative if unable to obtain ABG, recognizing that whilst providing accurate information about PaCO₂ and pH, it variably underestimates PaO₂ measurements. As a result, patient assessment can be based on pH and PCO₂ levels measured from earlobe or fingertip blood gases, together with SpO₂ by pulse oximetry `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- d. PaCO₂ may rise in susceptible individuals given oxygen therapy; therefore, a repeat ABG should be considered in those individuals `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### 2. Oxygen is a Drug and Thus Requires Prescription

**I. A specific oxygen prescription should be documented in the patient medical record and the drug chart (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**II. A target SpO₂ should be included as part of the prescription.**

*Practice points:*
- a. The minimum requirement for an oxygen prescription is specification of delivery device, range of flow rates that may be administered and target SpO₂ range `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- b. In its most detailed form, the prescription could include:
  - i. the delivery system and interface
  - ii. the target oxygen saturation range
  - iii. the range of flow rates that may be used for each delivery system
  - iv. specification of SpO₂ and FiO₂ (or flow rate) at which clinical review should be sought
  - v. if hNHF-O₂ therapy is utilized, the temperature setting, flow rate and FiO₂ of entrained oxygen `(Barnett et al., 2022)`
- c. Oxygen therapy should be initiated or increased if clinically required, prior to prescription. Increasing oxygen requirement indicates underlying disease deterioration, and should result in clinical review `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### 3. Recommended SpO₂ Targets (see Figure 2)

**I. An SpO₂ target of 88%–92% is recommended in exacerbations of COPD (Grade B), and other conditions associated with chronic respiratory failure (such as morbid obesity, obesity hypoventilation syndrome, bronchiectasis, cystic fibrosis, neuromuscular disease and chest wall deformities such as severe kyphoscoliosis) (Grade C). Where there is diagnostic uncertainty as to whether COPD is the primary cause of the exacerbation, it may be preferable to titrate oxygen therapy to the 88%–92% SpO₂ target range (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**II. In the presence of hypoxaemia in other acute medical conditions, oxygen should be administered to achieve a target SpO₂ range of 92%–96% (Grade B), with some evidence in support of a target range of 90%–94% (see Section C.3.IV) (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**III. An SpO₂ target of around 85% is recommended in patients previously exposed to bleomycin or in paraquat poisoning (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**IV. Patients with carbon monoxide poisoning, decompression sickness, cluster headaches and sickle cell crisis should receive normobaric hyperoxia. In the case of carbon monoxide poisoning, consideration should also be given to hyperbaric oxygen therapy (Grade B) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**V. Oxygen can be titrated using a closed-loop control system in which there is automated adjustment of the delivered oxygen concentration in response to continuous measurement of SpO₂. This results in a greater proportion of time within a prescribed target SpO₂ range compared to manual oxygen titration, in non-ventilated adult patients with acute illnesses using standard nasal cannulae, face mask or hNHF-O₂ (Grade B) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

*Practice points:*
- a. In the presence of COPD or conditions associated with chronic respiratory failure:
  - If SpO₂ ≥88%, oxygen therapy is not initially required
  - If SpO₂ <88%, oxygen can be administered via a 24% or 28% Venturi mask, at 1–2 L/min via nasal cannulae, or via a hNHF-O₂ device, and titrated to achieve the target SpO₂
  - If an SpO₂ target of ≥92% is considered in such patients, ABGs are required to be measured to exclude hypercapnia, before adopting this target
  - The avoidance of inappropriate high-concentration oxygen therapy may be facilitated by the provision of a COPD oxygen alert card, bracelet or warning in the medical record `(Barnett et al., 2022)`
- b. In the absence of COPD or known chronic respiratory failure:
  - If SpO₂ ≥92%, oxygen therapy is not routinely required
  - If SpO₂ is 85%–91%, oxygen can be initially instituted at 2–4 L/min via nasal cannulae or other suitable oxygen delivery method, and titrated to achieve the target SpO₂. In many situations, this range of oxygen saturations is unlikely to be associated with risk, although oxygen is commonly administered.
  - If SpO₂ <85%, oxygen can be initiated at 4 L/min via nasal cannulae, through a simple face mask at 5–10 L/min, a 100% non-rebreather reservoir mask at 15 L/min or hNHF-O₂ device (FiO₂ >0.35). The method of oxygen administration will depend on the SpO₂ level, with higher FiO₂ administered in response to increasingly more severe reductions in SpO₂. Oxygen is titrated to achieve the target SpO₂ as soon as practically possible `(Barnett et al., 2022)`
- c. If oximetry is not available or reliable SpO₂ cannot be determined and hypoxaemia is suspected, oxygen can be delivered at:
  - 24% or 28% Venturi mask, or 1–2 L/min via nasal cannulae in patients with acute exacerbations of COPD or conditions known to be associated with chronic respiratory failure
  - 2–4 L/min oxygen via nasal cannulae in patients who are not critically ill and life-threatening hypoxaemia is not suspected
  - 5–10 L/min via simple face mask, 15 L/min through a 100% non-rebreather reservoir mask or high-flow nasal cannulae (FiO₂ >0.35) in patients in whom life-threatening hypoxaemia is suspected `(Barnett et al., 2022)`
- d. Supplemental oxygen should not be used in normoxic patients in an attempt to protect against subsequent hypoxaemia, in the event of deterioration such as worsening gas exchange and/or alveolar ventilation:
  - A high SaO₂ due to supplemental oxygen obscures deterioration in SpO₂
  - In this situation, there is likely to be no major change in vital signs and no marked decrease in SpO₂ as assessed by pulse oximetry until a potentially life-threatening situation has developed. This may potentially delay recognition of the deterioration and thus provide a false reassurance that the patient is stable. At this late stage, further increasing oxygen therapy will have limited benefit while medical review and higher-level interventions, such as ventilation and transfer to an HDU or ICU, are undertaken `(Barnett et al., 2022)`

### 4. Delivery

**I. For most patients, standard nasal cannulae are the preferred method of oxygen delivery, with the flow rate varied to achieve the target oxygen saturation.**

*Practice point:* The FiO₂ levels delivered by the different delivery systems may vary considerably between patients and be influenced by a number of factors, including respiratory rate and whether the patient's mouth is open or closed. Approximate FiO₂ values delivered by different delivery systems are:
- Standard nasal cannulae can deliver an FiO₂ of 0.24–0.35 at an oxygen flow of 1–4 L/min
- Venturi masks can deliver an FiO₂ of 0.24–0.60
- hNHF-O₂ cannulae can deliver an FiO₂ of 0.21–1.0
- A simple face mask can deliver an FiO₂ of 0.35–0.60 at an oxygen flow of 5–10 L/min
- A 100% non-rebreather reservoir mask at 15 L/min can deliver an FiO₂ of >0.60 `(Barnett et al., 2022)`

**II. For simple face masks, flow rates of <5 L/min should be avoided due to the potential risk of carbon dioxide rebreathing (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**III. hNHF-O₂ devices deliver heated, humidified oxygen via wide-bore nasal cannulae at delivered concentrations up to 100% and at high-flow rates up to 70 L/min. The recommendations in this document only apply when oxygen is being delivered via this device and not, for example, when it is used to deliver warm, humidified air. It should be noted that there are other names used for these types of devices which perform the same function (e.g. high-flow nasal oxygen, high-flow nasal prong and high-flow nasal cannula). hNHF-O₂ should be considered in selected patients with severe, hypoxaemic respiratory failure (PaO₂:FiO₂ <300) (Grade B). Given the paucity of high-quality studies, current evidence does not support the routine use of hNHF-O₂ treatment in acute respiratory acidosis `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**IV. Fully conscious hypoxaemic patients should be allowed to position themselves according to their preference (Grade D). In some, but not all, patients, upright posture may result in improved oxygenation `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**V. In COPD and other conditions associated with chronic respiratory failure, if bronchodilator is required, the preferred method of administration is via metered dose inhaler (MDI) + spacer (or via air-driven nebulizer if considered necessary), with supplementary nasal oxygen continued as required (usually via nasal cannulae) for both modes of bronchodilator delivery (Grade B) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

*Practice points:*
- a. The administration of bronchodilator via an oxygen-driven nebulizer has the potential to cause an increase in PaCO₂. It has been recommended that if an oxygen-driven nebulizer is used, then its use is limited to 6 min `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- b. When hNHF-O₂ is used to treat serious illness with acute, hypoxaemic respiratory failure, ABG should be measured to exclude acute respiratory acidosis. It also allows calculation of PaO₂:FiO₂ ratio which is a useful indicator of severity of illness. Because hNHF-O₂ use is indicated for serious illness, recommendations below in Section B.5: 'High FiO₂ to achieve target SpO₂ indicates serious illness' should be considered `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

**VI. In asthma, if bronchodilator is required, the preferred method of delivery is by MDI + spacer. If a nebulizer is required, then it should be air-driven (Grade C). In both situations, if oxygen therapy is needed, it should continue via standard nasal cannulae during bronchodilator administration `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**VII. All aerosol-generating procedures, for example, hNHF-O₂ and non-invasive ventilatory support (non-invasive ventilation [NIV] and continuous positive airway pressure [CPAP]), pose a risk of transmission of viral infection to staff and patients. While these therapies offer significant benefits to some patients, there are often alternative approaches to management that have less risk of transmitting viral infection via aerosolization. This is of particular relevance during the 2019 SARS-CoV-2 pandemic where protection from aerosol spread remains standard practice `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**VIII. If hNHF-O₂ is used in a patient with suspected or known respiratory viral illness, including SARS-CoV-2, the patient must be fitted with an interface (e.g., face mask) to minimize leak and managed with the highest level of isolation available (class N-negative pressure room is optimal, single room with door closed is adequate) with five-piece personal protective equipment (PPE) (N95 or equivalent mask, gloves, goggles/glasses, gown, hat) precautions for healthcare personnel. Local facility infection control measures must be adopted (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**IX. NIV and CPAP are delivered by a mask. Patients with suspected or proven respiratory viral illness requiring CPAP/NIV should receive this via a circuit using a non-vented mask with a filtered external expiratory port. Staff should wear five-piece PPE, and the patient should be in the highest available level of isolation. Local facility infection control measures must be adopted (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

### 5. High FiO₂ to Achieve Target SpO₂ Indicates Serious Illness

**I. A high FiO₂ to maintain adequate SpO₂ is a clinical indicator of severe illness, and in this situation, there is limited capacity to increase FiO₂ to avoid life-threatening hypoxaemia should deterioration occur. Patients who need an estimated FiO₂ of ≥0.40, such as ≥6 L/min via a simple face mask, to maintain an adequate SpO₂, should receive senior clinician review and may require transfer to a facility such as HDU, where there are appropriate numbers of competent staff able to provide more intensive monitoring and therapy (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**II. Patients who need an estimated FiO₂ of ≥0.50, such as ≥8 L/min via a simple face mask, to maintain an adequate SpO₂, should receive ICU review and most will require a higher level of monitoring and supportive care which an ICU/HDU environment can provide (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

*Practice points:*
- a. A reduction in SpO₂ while the FiO₂ is maintained, or increasing FiO₂ requirements to maintain SpO₂, should lead to clinical review of the patient `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- b. For patients whose oxygen saturations improve with oxygen therapy to above the target oxygen saturation range, oxygen therapy can be reduced or stopped. Oxygen saturation monitoring should continue to allow detection of any subsequent deterioration of the underlying condition and the requirement to increase or resume oxygen therapy `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

**III. Early Warning Score (EWS) systems include a number of clinical parameters which together predict in-patient deterioration and subsequent life-threatening adverse patient events. EWS systems recommended for use in New Zealand and across Australia vary markedly.**
- It is recommended that EWS systems include scores that reflect (i) supplemental oxygen administration (i.e., scores increase as oxygen flow or FiO₂ delivery increases) and (ii) patient SpO₂ (i.e., scores increase as SpO₂ decreases) (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Current evidence suggests that the Queensland Adult Deterioration Detection System (Q-ADDS) outperforms other systems `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

*Practice point:*
- It is important that practitioners are familiar with the EWS system implemented in their healthcare organization. If no system is implemented, then the healthcare organization should consider using the Q-ADDS `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

### 6. Acute Respiratory Acidosis

**I. In patients with acute respiratory acidosis, in whom ABG show a pH <7.35 and PaCO₂ >45 mmHg, NIV or invasive ventilation should be considered. Target SpO₂ during NIV for acute respiratory acidosis should be 88%–92% (Grade A). COPD patients managed with NIV require regular review to assess response to treatment or deterioration, in which case they may need intubation. Assessment should be based on clinical and biochemical parameters (e.g., O₂ requirements, pH, etc.) (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**II. In patients in whom oxygen-induced hypercapnia is suspected, oxygen therapy should be titrated to maintain the 88%–92% target oxygen saturation range and not be abruptly stopped due to the risk of profound rebound hypoxaemia (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

*Practice point:*
- Reducing FiO₂ to appropriate levels may improve the acute respiratory acidosis. This should be determined by repeat ABG `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

**III. In patients with severe cardiogenic pulmonary oedema, CPAP should be considered (Grade A) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**IV. While NIV is not routinely recommended in acute hypoxaemic respiratory failure, its use may be considered in certain groups such as immunosuppressed patients with pulmonary infiltrates requiring ventilatory support (Grade C) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**V. It is recommended that patients receiving ventilatory support are located in a ward area such as close observation unit (COU), HDU or ICU (and may include a general ward), where it is essential that there are adequate numbers of staff experienced in ventilatory support to provide an appropriate level of monitoring, clinical expertise and titration of therapy (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

**VI. An individualized NIV treatment plan which covers prescription of therapy, escalation and de-escalation of NIV/CPAP, goals of therapy and 'ceiling of care' should be documented (Grade D) `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.**

# Oxygen: A New Look at an Old Therapy - Review Article (2019)

> **Source**: Beasley R, Mackle D, Young P. Oxygen: a new look at an old therapy. *Journal of the Royal Society of New Zealand*. 2019;49(2):126-142. doi:10.1080/03036758.2019.1566154

## Abstract
Oxygen is essential for aerobic respiration yet is potentially toxic. The capability to efficiently deliver sufficient oxygen for optimal cellular function, while minimising oxidant-induced tissue damage has been achieved through complex physiological processes which have developed through evolution. Until recently these physiological adaptations have been poorly appreciated in the practice of medicine, where high concentration oxygen therapy has been routinely administered to breathless patients, without consideration of the potential risks of oxygen toxicity. However, the recognition that both hypoxaemia and hyperoxaemia may cause harm has led to the recommendation that oxygen should only be prescribed if the patient is hypoxaemic and that its administration should be titrated to relieve hypoxaemia, and to avoid hyperoxaemia. It is also recommended that oxygen should be considered a drug which is prescribed for specific indications to achieve a specified arterial oxygen saturation range, and that the response is monitored to guide ongoing therapy `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

## Key Concepts

### Evolutionary Physiology of Oxygen
- Oxygen is essential for aerobic respiration yet potentially toxic, causing 'oxidative stress' when there is an imbalance between reactive oxygen species produced by aerobic respiration and the body's ability to detoxify these products `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Mammals have evolved respiratory systems to efficiently extract oxygen from the atmosphere while minimising oxidant-induced tissue damage `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- The concentration of oxygen in cells is a fraction of that in the atmosphere as a consequence of a physiological cascade in which serial step reductions in oxygen tension occur from ambient air through pulmonary, cardiac, macrovascular and microvascular systems into the cell `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Oxygen-derived free radicals are essential to and constantly generated in mitochondria; when not appropriately controlled they can damage macromolecules including lipids, proteins and nucleic acids `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Several enzymes play critical roles in protecting cells from oxygen free radicals: superoxide dismutase, catalase, vitamin E, vitamin C, glutathione `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### The Oxygen Haemoglobin Dissociation Curve
- Two key features reduce risk of both inadequate oxygen uptake/delivery AND excessive oxygen exposure at cellular level:
  1. **Flat top**: ensures haemoglobin is almost fully saturated; only minor reduction in arterial oxygen content despite severe cardiorespiratory disease resulting in profound falls in arterial oxygen tension `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
  2. **Steep slope**: capillary oxygen tension relatively preserved despite falling saturation of haemoglobin as oxygen delivered to peripheral tissues; pressure gradient main determinant of oxygen delivery `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Administration of high concentrations of oxygen to achieve non-physiologically high PaO₂ results in only small increases in arterial oxygen content, as oxygen poorly soluble in blood `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Hypoxaemia
- Defined as abnormally low level of oxygen in arterial blood, traditionally set at PaO₂ <60 mmHg or SaO₂ <90% `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Can be caused by: reduced inspired oxygen tension (altitude), reduced ventilatory drive (narcotic overdose), or impaired lung function (pneumonia) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- **Hypoxaemia is both a marker of risk of poor outcome due to severity of underlying disease AND a risk factor of poor outcome in its own right** `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- No absolute safe lower limit of PaO₂ or SaO₂ can be set; clinical effects depend on numerous factors including speed of onset, severity, duration, patient age, body temperature, disease chronicity, comorbidities, physiological factors, individual variation in susceptibility to hypoxaemia `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Brain most sensitive organ to effects of hypoxia; other vital organs (kidneys, liver, lungs, heart) may also be vulnerable depending on severity and associated clinical circumstances `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Sudden hypoxaemia leads to impaired mental function if SaO₂ falls rapidly to around 65%, consciousness lost at SaO₂ around 55% in normal subjects `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- In contrast, SpO₂ levels of 55% to 90% may be tolerated in some patients with chronic lung disease, obstructive sleep apnoea, obesity hypoventilation syndrome, or elite mountaineers who are acclimatised `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Hypoxaemia, defined as SpO₂ <90%, is an independent predictor of in-hospital mortality among patients admitted to hospital after emergency presentation, with 36% increased risk for SpO₂ between 86–89% compared with ≥90% `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Guidelines generally recommend use of supplementary oxygen to avoid hypoxaemia (PaO₂ <60 mmHg or SpO₂ <90%) in patients who are acutely unwell `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Recognising variability in accuracy of pulse oximetry, a higher SpO₂ threshold value of 92% can be used in clinical practice for this purpose `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Hyperoxaemia and High Inspired Oxygen Concentrations
- Defined as abnormally high level of oxygen in arterial blood, traditionally set at PaO₂ >120 mmHg (level at which SaO₂ is 100%) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Potential risks associated with high concentration oxygen therapy relate both to high FiO₂ delivered to lungs AND systemic effects of hyperoxaemia that results `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- At cellular level, exposure to high oxygen tension potentially toxic if production of reactive oxygen species in excess of physiological antioxidant defence capability `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Toxicity may be more pronounced in: organs such as lungs exposed to high FiO₂; situations such as reperfusion or cellular injury with heightened sensitivity to reactive oxygen species; conditions such as sepsis with increased reactive oxygen species formation `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- In baboons, exposure to FiO₂ >60% for a few days leads to pulmonary toxicity with alveolitis, hyaline membrane formation, cellular infiltration, pulmonary oedema and subsequently fibrosis `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- In healthy humans, breathing 30–50% oxygen for 45 h leads to lung injury with leakage of proteinaceous material into alveolar space `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Clinical recognition of this toxicity often difficult because clinical assessment relatively insensitive method of identifying initial oxygen-induced pathological injury, and clinical features of oxygen-induced lung injury overlap with those of underlying lung disease `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Oxygen therapy may 'prime' the lung for subsequent worsened injury; has led to concept of permissive hypoxaemia, in which lower oxygen tension may be accepted as long as no overt manifestations of impaired tissue perfusion, to minimise risk of deleterious ventilation strategies `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Physiological Effects of Hyperoxaemia (Table 1)
| System | Effects | Risks | Citation |
|--------|---------|-------|----------|
| **Respiratory** | Decreased ventilation (minimal) | Worsened ventilation/perfusion matching; Absorption atelectasis | `(Beasley et al., 2019)` |
| **Cardiovascular** | Reduced cardiac output; Reduced coronary blood flow; Increased blood pressure; Increased peripheral resistance | Myocardial ischaemia (in context of decreased haematocrit) | `(Beasley et al., 2019)` |
| **Metabolic** | Decreased 2,3-DPG; Decreased CO₂ carriage (Haldane effect); Increased reactive oxygen species | - | `(Beasley et al., 2019)` |
| **Neurological** | Decreased cerebral blood flow | - | `(Beasley et al., 2019)` |
| **Renal** | Reduced renal blood flow | - | `(Beasley et al., 2019)` |

### High FiO₂ and Hyperoxaemia Lead to Complex Respiratory Effects
- Depending on underlying chronic and/or acute respiratory disorders, may include:
  - Reduction in respiratory drive
  - Reversal of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction
  - Absorption atelectasis
- Result: reduced alveolar ventilation
- Together with Haldane effect (unloading of CO₂ by haemoglobin in hyperoxic conditions), these respiratory effects lead to increase in arterial partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO₂) which may be harmful if marked `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Cardiovascular Effects of Hyperoxaemia
- In response to hyperoxaemia, cardiovascular system reduces oxygen delivery to tissues by:
  - Decreasing cardiac output
  - Increasing systemic vascular resistance and blood pressure
  - Reducing coronary arterial blood flow
- Result: paradoxical reduction in myocardial oxygen consumption `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Delayed Recognition of Deterioration
- Use of high concentration oxygen in a breathless patient who is not hypoxaemic in an attempt to protect against future hypoxaemia should their condition worsen has potential to delay recognition of such deterioration `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Delay may occur because unlikely to be any major change in vital signs or marked decrease in oxygen saturations as assessed by pulse oximetry until potentially life-threatening situation has developed `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- At this stage, less opportunity to intervene, either by further increasing oxygen therapy, or transferring to HDU/ICU to provide more intensive management `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Pulse oximetry can detect hypoxaemia but cannot differentiate between hyperoxaemia and normoxaemia (e.g., PaO₂ of 100 and 300 mmHg will both lead to SpO₂ reading of 100%) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Titration of Oxygen Therapy to Target Arterial Oxygen Saturation Range
- Appreciation of potential risks of both hypoxaemia and hyperoxaemia has led to calls for oxygen to be considered a medication that is:
  - Prescribed and administered for specific indications
  - Delivered through specific device
  - At specific flow rates to achieve documented target oxygen saturation range
  - With regular monitoring of patient's response to guide ongoing therapy `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Concept of titrating oxygen therapy to within a target oxygen saturation range: colloquially referred to as **'swimming between the flags'** `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- This concept has been researched in randomised controlled trials comparing titration of oxygen to within target saturation range (to avoid both hypoxaemia and hyperoxaemia) with standard practice of liberal oxygen use across range of different medical conditions `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

## Key Randomised Controlled Trial Evidence

### COPD Exacerbations (Austin et al., 2010)
- Randomised controlled trial of liberal versus titrated oxygen therapy in patients with severe exacerbations of COPD managed by Ambulance Service in transit to hospital `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- **Titrated oxygen regimen**: titrated supplementary oxygen if required to achieve SpO₂ 88–92% + bronchodilators delivered by air-driven nebuliser `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- **Liberal oxygen regimen**: high flow oxygen therapy at 8–11 L/min + bronchodilators delivered by nebulisation with oxygen flows of 6–8 L/min `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- **Results**: Titrated oxygen reduced mortality by 58% for all patients included in study (intention to treat analysis) and by 78% for all patients with confirmed COPD `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- From these data, treating 14 patients with liberal oxygen would result in one avoidable death `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- High flow oxygen regimen associated with more severe respiratory acidosis and hypercapnia (raised PaCO₂), which likely contributed to mortality risk as most deaths due to respiratory failure `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Key finding: just over half of patients in titrated oxygen arm received high flow oxygen therapy at some point in pre-hospital treatment, likely as result of entrenched culture that 'more oxygen is better' in breathless distressed patient `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Other Respiratory Conditions
Since the COPD trial, studies have shown similar physiological responses of increase in PaCO₂ with high flow oxygen therapy across range of other acute respiratory conditions:
- **Asthma** (Rodrigo et al., 2003; Perrin et al., 2011) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`
- **Pneumonia** (Wijesinghe et al., 2012) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`
- **Obesity hypoventilation syndrome** (Wijesinghe et al., 2011) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`

In these studies, high flow oxygen increased PaCO₂ compared with breathing room air or titrated oxygen therapy to within target SpO₂ range, suggesting conservative oxygen administration across all acute and chronic respiratory conditions in which hypoxaemia may be present and oxygen therapy prescribed may reduce harm `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

**Likely mechanisms**: worsening ventilation/perfusion mismatch as result of release of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction, and reduction in ventilatory drive, both of which reduce alveolar ventilation, leading to increase in PaCO₂ `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis (Chu et al., 2018)
- Identified 25 randomised controlled trials of liberal versus conservative oxygen therapy across wide range of conditions including sepsis, critical illness, stroke, trauma, myocardial infarction, cardiac arrest and patients who had emergency surgery `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- **Compared with conservative oxygen regimen, liberal oxygen regimen increased**:
  - In-hospital mortality at 30 days by about 20% (relative risk 1.21, 95% CI 1.03–1.43, I² = 0%, high quality) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`
  - Mortality at long term follow-up by about 10% (relative risk 1.10, 95% CI 1.00–1.20, I² = 0%, high quality) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`
- Mortality findings robust in trial sequential, subgroup and sensitivity analyses `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- From meta-analysis, number needed to harm resulting in one death using liberal approach approximately 71 `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Findings of major global public health importance in view of near ubiquitous use of oxygen in acutely ill patients `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Findings parallel other fields of study in which overly aggressive treatment of physiological parameters promotes harm: transfusion thresholds, glucose management in critically ill patients, normalisation of PaCO₂ in ARDS `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

### Paediatric Considerations
- Management of infants with bronchiolitis to SpO₂ target of 90% or higher as safe and clinically effective as one of 94% or higher `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Increased mortality observed with administration of 100% oxygen compared with room air during neonatal resuscitation of babies mostly at term or near term `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- Also increased mortality with use of oxygen saturation target of 85–89% compared with 91–95%, when administered to extremely preterm infants `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.
- This U-shaped curve of mortality risk has led to guidelines recommending that babies resuscitated at term should be started on air (21%), and initiation of resuscitation in preterm newborns with low oxygen concentration (21–30%) `(Beasley et al., 2019)`.

## Guideline Recommendations Summary (TSANZ 2015/2022)

### Target Oxygen Saturation Ranges
| Patient Group | When to Administer Oxygen | Target SpO₂ Range | Citation |
|--------------|-------------------------|----------------|----------|
| COPD and conditions associated with chronic respiratory failure | SpO₂ <88% | 88–92% | `(Barnett et al., 2022)` |
| Other acute medical conditions | SpO₂ <92% | 92–96% | `(Barnett et al., 2022)` |

### High FiO₂ Indicates Serious Illness
| Requirement | Action | Citation |
|------------|--------|----------|
| FiO₂ ≥0.40 (such as ≥6 L/min via simple face mask) to maintain adequate SpO₂ | Senior clinician review | `(Barnett et al., 2022)` |
| FiO₂ ≥0.50 (such as ≥8 L/min via simple face mask) to maintain adequate SpO₂ | ICU review | `(Barnett et al., 2022)` |

### Other Key Recommendations
- Pulse oximetry should be available in all clinical situations in which oxygen is used `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Arterial blood gas measurements should be considered in critically ill patients, patients with SpO₂ <92%, deteriorating SpO₂ requiring increased FiO₂, patients at risk of hypercapnia, breathless patients in whom reliable oximetry signal cannot be obtained `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Oxygen saturation measured by pulse oximetry should be considered a 'vital sign' and documented with other signs in patient assessment and management `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- An oxygen prescription should be documented in patient records and drug chart `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- In COPD and other conditions associated with chronic respiratory failure, preferred method of bronchodilator administration is air-driven nebuliser or metered dose inhaler ± spacer `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- For most patients, standard nasal cannulae are preferred method of oxygen delivery, with flow rate varied to achieve target oxygen saturation `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- In patients with hypercapnic respiratory failure (arterial pH <7.35 and PaCO₂ >45 mmHg), NIV or invasive ventilation should be considered. COPD patients with pH <7.25 managed with NIV require intensive monitoring with low threshold for intubation `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.
- Patients receiving ventilator support should be located in area such as HDU, ICU, close observation unit or monitored bed unit, where adequate numbers of staff experienced in ventilatory support to provide appropriate level of monitoring and titration of therapy `(Barnett et al., 2022)`.

## Practical Implementation: 'Swimming Between the Flags'

The concept of administering oxygen only if necessary to relieve hypoxaemia, but at an FiO₂ that does not lead to hyperoxaemia:
[LOWER FLAG] SpO₂ <92% → Administer oxygen
↓
Titrate to target
↓
[TARGET ZONE] SpO₂ 92–96% (or 88–92% if at risk of hypercapnia)
↓
[UPPER FLAG] SpO₂ >96% → Reduce/withhold oxygen

**Key principles** `(Beasley et al., 2019)`:
1. Oxygen is a drug: prescribe for specific indications
2. Titrate to target: avoid both hypoxaemia AND hyperoxaemia
3. Monitor response: adjust therapy based on SpO₂ and clinical assessment
4. Recognise limitations: pulse oximetry cannot detect hyperoxaemia; ABG required when indicated
