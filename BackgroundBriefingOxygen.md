# Background Briefing: Oxygen Therapy in Acute Care (Non-ICU)

**Updated from source documents | Evidence base: BTS 2017, TSANZ 2022, ACI COVID algorithm, Beasley et al. 2019, Cousins et al. 2020**

---

## Philosophy of Oxygen Therapy

> *"Oxygen is a treatment for hypoxaemia, not breathlessness."*
> — BTS 2017 / TSANZ 2022

Oxygen has not been proven to relieve breathlessness in non-hypoxaemic patients. The goal is to prescribe oxygen to a **target saturation range** — avoiding both hypoxaemia *and* hyperoxaemia — and to monitor the patient's response continuously.

This is colloquially described as **"swimming between the flags"** (Beasley et al. 2019, TSANZ 2022): administer oxygen only if required, titrated to relieve hypoxaemia without inducing hyperoxaemia.

---

## Why Both Extremes Are Harmful

<details>
<summary><strong>Risks of Hypoxaemia</strong></summary>

- Hypoxaemia ($\text{SpO}_2 < 90\%$ or $\text{PaO}_2 < 60\,\text{mmHg}$) causes tissue hypoxia, organ dysfunction and death
- The brain is the most sensitive organ; mental impairment begins around $\text{SaO}_2 \approx 65\%$; consciousness is lost around $55\%$ in healthy adults
- $\text{SpO}_2 < 90\%$ is an independent predictor of in-hospital mortality (Goodacre et al. 2006)
- Hypoxaemia is both a **marker of disease severity** and an independent **risk factor** for poor outcomes

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Risks of Hyperoxaemia and High FiO₂</strong></summary>

- Respiratory: worsened V/Q mismatch (release of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction), absorption atelectasis, direct pulmonary toxicity, raised $\text{PaCO}_2$
- Cardiovascular: increased systemic vascular resistance and blood pressure, reduced coronary blood flow, reduced cardiac output
- Cerebrovascular: reduced cerebral blood flow
- Increased reactive oxygen species → oxidative tissue damage
- **Masks deterioration**: high $\text{FiO}_2$ delays $\text{SpO}_2$ falls, delaying recognition of clinical deterioration and escalation (Beasley et al. 2007)
- A 2018 meta-analysis (Chu et al.) of 25 RCTs across 16,037 patients showed liberal versus conservative oxygen increased in-hospital mortality by **21%** (RR 1.21, 95% CI 1.03–1.43)

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Oxygen-Induced Hypercapnia</strong></summary>

High-concentration oxygen causes $\text{PaCO}_2$ rise in susceptible patients through multiple mechanisms:
- V/Q mismatch (dominant mechanism in COPD): release of hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction increases perfusion to poorly ventilated alveoli
- Reduced ventilatory drive
- Haldane effect: oxyhaemoglobin carries less $\text{CO}_2$
- Absorption atelectasis
- Increased airway resistance (higher oxygen density)
- Rebreathing at low flow rates through face masks

This is not exclusive to COPD — observed also in asthma, pneumonia, obesity hypoventilation and cystic fibrosis exacerbations.

</details>

## Target SpO₂ Ranges

| Patient Group | Target SpO₂ | Initiate O₂ When |
|---|---|---|
| Most acute medical patients | **94–98%** (BTS) or **92–96%** (TSANZ) | SpO₂ < 94% (BTS) or < 92% (TSANZ) |
| COPD, CF, morbid obesity, neuromuscular/chest wall disease, bronchiectasis — **at risk of hypercapnia** | **88–92%** | SpO₂ < 88% |
| Palliative care | SpO₂ consistently < 90% *or* patient reports benefit | Clinical judgement |
| Paraquat/bleomycin poisoning | **85–88%** | SpO₂ < 85% |
| Carbon monoxide poisoning | **100%** (reservoir mask regardless of oximetry) | Always |
| Cluster headache | Aim relief; use ≥12 L/min reservoir mask | Clinical presentation |

> **NSW Health context (ACI COVID algorithm):** Target 92–96% for most acute inpatients; 88–92% for patients at risk of hypercapnia. These align with BTS guidance and apply broadly beyond COVID.

**Important caveats:**
- Older adults may have physiologically lower baseline $\text{SpO}_2$ (mean ~95.5% in those >64 years; BTS)
- A sudden fall of **≥3%** within the target range warrants clinical assessment, even if still "in range"
- Target ranges must be **prescribed and documented** for all admitted patients

---

## Oxygen as a Prescribed Medication

Oxygen is a drug. Under NSW Health policy it is a **Schedule 4 medication** requiring a prescription. Key principles:

- Prescribe to a **target saturation range**, not a fixed flow rate or FiO₂
- Document: device, flow rate range, target SpO₂ (upper and lower), criteria for escalation
- Nurses may initiate oxygen emergently under deterioration protocols, but must escalate and ensure retrospective documentation
- **Never withhold oxygen in an emergency** because a prescription is not yet written — document retrospectively

Evidence shows that prescription rates remain poor: only 57% of UK inpatients on supplemental oxygen had a valid prescription in 2015 (BTS audit). Australian data show similar deficits (Cousins et al. 2020, Wijesinghe et al. 2010).

## Device Selection and Flow Rates

| Device | Approximate FiO₂ | Flow Rate | Indications |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nasal cannulae** | 0.24–0.35 | 1–4 L/min (low-medium) | Preferred for most patients; comfortable, allows eating/talking, avoids rebreathing |
| **Nasal cannulae (higher flow)** | up to ~0.50 | 5–6 L/min | Medium concentration; patient-preferred over face masks |
| **Simple face mask (Hudson mask)** | 0.35–0.60 | 5–10 L/min (minimum 5 L/min) | Medium-high concentration; avoid <5 L/min (rebreathing risk) |
| **Venturi mask** | 0.24–0.60 (colour-coded) | Device-specific | Precise FiO₂; preferred when controlled concentration essential; increase flow by 50% if RR >30 |
| **High-concentration reservoir mask** | >0.60 | 15 L/min | Initial management of severe hypoxaemia ($\text{SpO}_2 < 85\%$), critical illness, carbon monoxide poisoning |
| **Humidified High-Flow Nasal Cannulae (hNHF-O₂ / HFNC)** | 0.21–1.0 | up to 60–70 L/min | Severe hypoxaemic respiratory failure; superior alternative to reservoir mask; requires humidification |

**Key device rules:**
- For **COPD/hypercapnia risk**: start 24% Venturi at 2–3 L/min, or 28% Venturi at 4 L/min, or nasal cannulae at 1–2 L/min
- Nasal cannulae are preferred over face masks for most ward patients (comfort, patient compliance, cost, no rebreathing risk)
- HFNC is the preferred alternative to reservoir mask in acute hypoxaemic respiratory failure **without hypercapnia** (BTS, TSANZ)
- **Do not use bubble humidifiers** — no clinical benefit, infection risk (BTS Grade D)
- Humidification is not required for low-flow oxygen or short-term high-flow; reasonable after >24 hours of high-flow or with patient discomfort

## Initiating Oxygen Therapy

```
Is the patient critically ill?
  YES → Reservoir mask 15 L/min; manage as per resuscitation guidelines
  NO  ↓

At risk of hypercapnic respiratory failure?
  YES → Target 88–92%; start Venturi 24–28% or nasal cannulae 1–2 L/min; obtain ABG
  NO  ↓

SpO₂ < 85%? → Reservoir mask 15 L/min; escalate
SpO₂ 85–91%? → Nasal cannulae 2–4 L/min; titrate to target
SpO₂ ≥ 92%? → Oxygen not routinely required; monitor
```

*Adapted from BTS Chart 2 and TSANZ 2022 algorithm*

## Titration and Monitoring

- Allow at least **5 minutes** at each flow rate before adjusting further (except for sudden large SpO₂ falls ≥3%)
- Adjust upward if SpO₂ below target; adjust downward if SpO₂ above target
- **Do not use oxygen to "protect" a normoxaemic patient** from future deterioration — this masks early warning signs
- Record $\text{SpO}_2$ and delivery device/flow on **every** observation chart entry
- Stable patients: minimum **four observations daily** (BTS)
- NEWS 7+: consider continuous monitoring; HDU/ICU referral

**When to obtain ABG:**
- Critically ill patients
- $\text{SpO}_2 < 92\%$ or requiring supplemental oxygen to maintain target
- Deteriorating SpO₂ requiring increased FiO₂
- Any patient at risk of hypercapnia
- When diagnosis, severity assessment or management depend on blood gas values
- Venous blood gas: provides rapid pH, lactate, $\text{K}^+$, $\text{Na}^+$, glucose — but **unreliable for PaCO₂** (95th percentile bias up to 21 mmHg above arterial; TSANZ 2022); ABG required to exclude hypercapnia

**Signs of deterioration requiring escalation:**
- Increasing respiratory rate (especially >30 — most sensitive vital sign)
- Increasing FiO₂ requirement to maintain target SpO₂
- Rising NEWS/EWS score
- Signs of $\text{CO}_2$ retention: drowsiness, headache, flushed face, bounding pulse, flapping tremor

---

## Escalation Thresholds

| FiO₂ Required | Action |
|---|---|
| ≥ 0.40 (≥ 6 L/min via face mask) | **Senior clinician review**; consider HDU |
| ≥ 0.50 (≥ 8 L/min via face mask) | **ICU review**; most require higher-level support |

> If target saturations cannot be maintained despite appropriate adjustments, escalate without delay. Do not assume device failure without checking.

## COPD and Hypercapnic Respiratory Failure — Special Considerations

<details>
<summary><strong>Expand for detail</strong></summary>

- Target SpO₂ **88–92%** pending ABG results (Grade A evidence for COPD)
- Avoid PaO₂ > 10 kPa (75 mmHg) — associated with worse respiratory acidosis
- Use 24% or 28% Venturi mask (or nasal cannulae 1–2 L/min)
- If respiratory rate >30: **increase Venturi mask flow by up to 50%** (increasing flow into Venturi does not change FiO₂, only total gas flow)
- Check blood gases within **30–60 minutes** of initiating or changing therapy
- If pH < 7.35 and PaCO₂ > 6.0 kPa after 30 minutes of appropriate treatment: consider NIV
- **Oxygen alert cards**: patients with prior hypercapnic failure should carry a card specifying their target range and device; ambulance and ED staff should follow card guidance
- **Nebuliser therapy**: use MDI ± spacer (preferred); if nebuliser required, use **air-driven** with supplemental nasal cannulae oxygen; oxygen-driven nebulisers in COPD should be limited to **6 minutes**
- **Do not suddenly stop oxygen** if hypercapnia is suspected — step down gradually to avoid rebound hypoxaemia (the alveolar gas equation means $\text{PaO}_2$ falls faster than $\text{PaCO}_2$ corrects)

Other conditions with hypercapnia risk (treat like COPD):
- Morbid obesity (BMI > 40), obesity hypoventilation syndrome
- Cystic fibrosis
- Severe kyphoscoliosis / ankylosing spondylitis
- Neuromuscular disease (Guillain-Barré, MND, myopathies)
- Fixed airflow obstruction (bronchiectasis)
- Long-term home ventilation users

</details>

## Weaning and Discontinuation

1. Reduce flow incrementally when SpO₂ is **above** target range or has been stable in upper zone for 4–8 hours
2. Most convalescent patients wean to **2 L/min nasal cannulae** before cessation
3. Patients with hypercapnia risk may step to **1 L/min or 0.5 L/min**; or 24% Venturi at 2 L/min as lowest step
4. Stop oxygen when:
   - Patient is **clinically stable**
   - $\text{SpO}_2$ within or above target range **on two consecutive observations breathing air**
5. Monitor $\text{SpO}_2$ for **5 minutes** after stopping; recheck at **1 hour**
6. If $\text{SpO}_2$ falls below target: restart lowest effective concentration; reattempt discontinuation later
7. If higher concentration required to re-achieve same target: **clinical review required** — this indicates deterioration
8. Leave the target range prescription active — episodic hypoxaemia after weaning does not require automatic resumption of continuous oxygen

## Common Failure Modes (Evidence-Based)

| Failure Mode | Corrective Action |
|---|---|
| Oxygen given for breathlessness without confirming hypoxaemia | Check SpO₂ first; oxygen does not relieve breathlessness in non-hypoxaemic patients |
| High-flow oxygen "just in case" masking deterioration | Titrate to target range; use SpO₂ as active monitoring tool |
| No prescribed target range on drug chart | All admitted patients must have a documented target range at admission |
| Assuming deterioration without checking device fit | Check device position, connections and oxygen supply before escalating |
| Oxygen-driven nebuliser in COPD causing hypercapnia | Use air-driven nebuliser; limit oxygen-driven use to 6 minutes |
| Sudden cessation of oxygen in hypercapnic patient | Step down gradually — rebound hypoxaemia can be life-threatening |
| Administering oxygen to normoxaemic AMI or stroke patient | Evidence of harm; only give oxygen if SpO₂ < 94% (AMI) or < 94% (stroke) |
| Knowledge gaps in clinicians (Cousins et al. 2020) | Over one-third of surveyed clinicians unaware of the TSANZ guideline; target saturation correctly identified by only ~37% across clinical scenarios |

## Specific Conditions: Quick Reference

| Condition | Target SpO₂ | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pneumonia | 94–98% | High-flow O₂ raises PaCO₂ even in pneumonia (Wijesinghe et al. 2012) |
| Acute asthma | 94–98% | 100% O₂ increases PaCO₂ and worsens PEF; titrated O₂ preferred |
| Acute heart failure / pulmonary oedema | 94–98% (88–92% if hypercapnia risk) | Consider CPAP if not responding to standard therapy |
| Pulmonary embolism | 94–98% (88–92% if COPD) | Most minor PE patients are not hypoxaemic |
| Stroke | 94–98% | Avoid routine O₂ in normoxaemic mild-moderate stroke; monitor 4-hourly |
| Myocardial infarction / ACS | 94–98% only if hypoxaemic | No O₂ benefit in normoxaemia; evidence of harm (increased infarct size) |
| Postoperative / perioperative | 94–98% (88–92% if hypercapnia risk) | Hyperoxaemia not routinely recommended |
| Palliative care | SpO₂ < 90% consistently | Opioids and fan (non-pharmacological) preferred for breathlessness first |
| Pneumothorax (observed, no drain) | 94–98% (reservoir mask) | High-flow O₂ accelerates pneumothorax absorption |
| Sickle cell crisis | 94–98% or patient's usual baseline | Pulse oximetry may underestimate true SaO₂ in sickle cell |

## Pulse Oximetry: Limitations Clinicians Must Know

- Accurate to within 1–2% of directly measured $\text{SaO}_2$ in most patients
- **Falsely normal** in: carbon monoxide poisoning (carboxyhaemoglobin reads as oxyhaemoglobin), methaemoglobinaemia, severe anaemia
- **Less accurate** in: dark skin pigmentation (may overestimate by >4%), poor peripheral perfusion, hypotension, nail varnish/false nails, hypothermia, movement artefact, severe hypercapnia, sepsis
- **Does not detect**: hypercapnia, acidosis, anaemia
- A **normal $\text{SpO}_2$ on supplemental oxygen does not exclude** rising $\text{PaCO}_2$ or metabolic derangement
- Document the delivery device and flow rate **alongside every SpO₂ reading**

---

## Documentation Requirements

Every oxygen therapy record must include:
- Device type (using standard abbreviations: N, V24, V28, SM, RM, HFN, TM — see BTS Table 5)
- Flow rate (L/min)
- $\text{FiO}_2$ if known
- Target SpO₂ range
- Actual SpO₂ at time of recording
- Time and date of each change
- Clinician signature at each drug round (or "A" for air if patient not on oxygen)

---

## NSW Health Policy Reminders

- Oxygen is a **Schedule 4 medication** under NSW Health policy requiring a valid prescription
- Nurses may initiate or increase oxygen under deterioration protocols in emergencies; this must be **documented retrospectively**
- Escalate to the JMO/senior clinician if:
  - FiO₂ ≥ 0.40 required to maintain target (senior review)
  - FiO₂ ≥ 0.50 required (ICU review)
  - Target saturations cannot be maintained with device adjustments
  - Patient shows increasing work of breathing or persistent hypoxaemia
- The ACI NSW stepped respiratory support algorithm (COVID guideline) applies broadly: standard oxygen → HFNC → CPAP → NIV → ICU/intubation, with limits of therapy discussed and documented at each step
- Patients admitted to acute wards requiring oxygen should have **goals of care and escalation limits** discussed and documented
